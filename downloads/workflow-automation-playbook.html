01

Audit Your Current Workflows

Before you automate anything, understand what you're working with

The biggest mistake businesses make with automation is jumping straight to solutions before understanding the problem. A thorough workflow audit reveals where your time is actually going — and the results often surprise people.

How to Map Your Workflows

  1. List every repetitive process your team performs weekly or monthly. Don't filter yet — capture everything from "send Monday report" to "process new client paperwork."
  2. Track time for one week. Ask team members to log how long each process takes. Real data beats estimates every time.
  3. Document the steps. For your top 10 time-consuming processes, write out every single step. Include the systems used, the people involved, and where handoffs happen.
  4. Identify pain points. Where do errors occur? Where do things get stuck? Which steps require a specific person (creating a bottleneck)?

02

Score and Prioritise

Focus your resources on the highest-value opportunities first

Not all processes are equal candidates for automation. Some will deliver massive ROI with minimal effort; others are complex to automate and deliver modest returns. The priority matrix below helps you score each process objectively and rank them by impact.

Priority Matrix Scoring

Score each process from 1 (low) to 5 (high) across four criteria:

  • Time Impact (1–5): How many hours per week does this process consume? (1 = under 1 hour, 5 = 8+ hours)
  • Error Cost (1–5): How expensive are mistakes in this process? (1 = minor inconvenience, 5 = significant financial loss or customer impact)
  • Complexity to Automate (1–5): How straightforward is the automation? (1 = highly complex, 5 = simple and well-defined). Note: Higher score = easier to automate.
  • Business Impact (1–5): How much does this process affect revenue, customer experience, or growth? (1 = back-office only, 5 = directly revenue-impacting)

Composite Score Calculation

Priority Formula
Composite Score = Time Impact + Error Cost + Ease of Automation + Business Impact
Maximum possible score: 20 | Automate highest scores first

Worked Example: 5 Common Processes Scored

How to Read This

Invoice Processing scores highest because it's time-consuming, error-prone, easy to automate, and directly affects cash flow. Email Follow-ups score second because of their outsized revenue impact — consistent follow-up can increase close rates by 30%+. Start with the highest-scoring process and work your way down.

04

Implementation Timeline

What to expect during a typical 4-week automation project

Here's what a standard medium-complexity automation project looks like from start to finish. This timeline assumes a single workflow — more complex, multi-workflow projects will take longer.

W1

Week 1: Discovery and Mapping

  • Kick-off meeting to review your workflow audit and priorities
  • Detailed process mapping: every step, every decision point, every system
  • Identify integration requirements and confirm API availability
  • Define success metrics (time saved, error reduction, speed improvement)
  • Agree on scope, timeline, and deliverables

Your involvement: 2–3 hours for meetings and process walkthroughs

W2

Week 2: Design and Approval

  • Workflow design document: visual diagram showing the complete automation
  • Integration architecture: how systems will connect and data will flow
  • Error handling strategy: what happens when things go wrong
  • Review session with your team to confirm the design
  • Sign-off before building begins

Your involvement: 1–2 hours for review and approval

W3

Week 3: Build and Initial Testing

  • Build the automation workflow in the chosen platform
  • Configure integrations with your existing systems
  • Unit testing: verify each step works correctly in isolation
  • End-to-end testing: run complete workflows with test data
  • Fix issues and refine based on test results

Your involvement: 30 minutes for test access and feedback

W4

Week 4: Go-Live, Training, and Handover

  • Final user acceptance testing with real data
  • Go-live: switch from manual process to automated workflow
  • Team training session (typically 1 hour)
  • Documentation handover: how it works, what to monitor, who to contact
  • Post-launch monitoring to catch any teething issues

Your involvement: 2 hours for testing and training

Post

Post-Launch: Monitor and Optimise

  • Week 1–2 post-launch: daily monitoring for issues
  • Week 3–4: fine-tuning based on real-world performance
  • Month 2: review metrics against targets, optimise where needed
  • Ongoing: periodic reviews as your business processes evolve

Your involvement: Minimal — just flag any issues you notice

Total Time Investment

Your team typically spends 6–8 hours total across a 4-week project. The rest is handled by the implementation team. This is designed to minimise disruption to your business while delivering maximum value.

05

Evaluate Your Options

Make an informed decision about how to proceed

10 Questions to Ask Any Automation Vendor

☐ 1. What platforms do you work with? Ensure they're not locked into one tool and can recommend the best fit for your needs.
☐ 2. Can you share examples of similar projects? Look for experience in your industry or with your specific systems.
☐ 3. What does ongoing support include? Clarify whether maintenance, monitoring, and updates are included or extra.
☐ 4. How do you handle data privacy and security? Especially important if you handle customer data, financial records, or health information.
☐ 5. What happens if something breaks? Understand response times, escalation procedures, and error handling approaches.
☐ 6. Do we own the automation workflows? Ensure you're not locked in. Can you take the workflows if you part ways?
☐ 7. What's the realistic timeline? Be wary of promises that sound too fast. Good automation takes proper planning and testing.
☐ 8. How do you measure success? Look for vendors who define clear metrics before building, not vague promises afterwards.
☐ 9. What are the ongoing costs beyond implementation? Factor in platform subscriptions, hosting, and maintenance fees.
☐ 10. Can we start small and scale? The best vendors encourage starting with one process and expanding based on results.

DIY vs Expert: Decision Framework

Build It Yourself If...

  • The workflow is simple (2–3 steps, single trigger)
  • Someone on your team is comfortable with no-code tools
  • It's not mission-critical (errors won't cause financial loss)
  • You have time to learn and experiment
  • Budget is very limited (under R15,000)
  • You want to build internal automation capability

Bring In an Expert If...

  • Multiple systems need to integrate
  • The process is mission-critical (invoicing, customer-facing)
  • Data accuracy and reliability are essential
  • You want it done quickly and correctly the first time
  • Custom logic or AI capabilities are needed
  • You'd rather focus your team on core business activities

Many businesses take a hybrid approach: DIY for simple automations (email follow-ups, basic notifications) and expert help for complex, high-stakes integrations (invoice processing, multi-system data sync, customer onboarding).

Real-World Results: 3 Mini Case Studies

Case Study 1: Marketing Agency

Automated Reporting

The Situation: A 15-person Cape Town marketing agency managed reporting for 30+ clients. Every Monday, two team members spent the entire morning pulling data from Google Analytics, Facebook Ads, Google Ads, and HubSpot, then formatting individual reports for each client. Total time: 10+ hours per week across the team.

The Solution: Automated report generation that pulls data from all platforms overnight every Sunday, populates client-specific templates, generates branded PDFs, and emails them to each client before 8am on Monday.

Case Study 2: Legal Firm

Client Onboarding

The Situation: A boutique legal practice onboarded 8–12 new clients per month. Each onboarding required: conflict check, engagement letter generation, document collection (ID, proof of address, relevant case documents), matter file creation, and introduction to the assigned attorney. The process took an average of 2 days per client, with frequent delays when documents were missing or incomplete.

The Solution: An automated onboarding workflow triggered when a new matter is opened in their practice management system. The system generates the engagement letter from templates, sends a secure document collection form to the client, tracks submissions with automated reminders, creates the matter file when all documents are received, and notifies the assigned attorney with a complete brief.

The Result:

Every client now receives the same professional onboarding experience, regardless of which partner handles their matter. No more missing documents, no more delays, no more inconsistency.

Case Study 3: E-commerce Business

Order Processing & Inventory

The Situation: An online retailer selling across their Shopify store, Takealot, and two other marketplace platforms processed 200+ orders per week. Staff manually checked inventory across platforms, updated stock levels after each sale, and reconciled orders with their accounting system daily. During peak sales periods, errors spiked: overselling (items sold on one platform after selling out on another), incorrect shipment details, and accounting mismatches.

The Solution: A real-time inventory sync across all four sales channels, with automated order processing that creates fulfilment tasks in their warehouse system and syncs completed orders to their accounting platform. Automated alerts trigger when stock drops below reorder thresholds.

The Result:
