n8n vs Make.com: Which Automation Platform Is Right for You?

October 2024 8 min read

If you're looking into workflow automation, two platforms come up in almost every conversation: n8n and Make.com. Both are powerful. Both have passionate communities. And both can handle serious business automation.

But they're built on fundamentally different philosophies, and the right choice depends entirely on your situation. We use both at Eight38 — and we have a clear preference for serious work. Here's our honest breakdown.

The Quick Overview

n8n is an open-source, source-available workflow automation platform. You can self-host it on your own server or use their cloud service. It's built for people who want maximum control, flexibility, and the ability to handle complex workflows.

Make.com (formerly Integromat) is a cloud-based visual automation platform. It's known for its intuitive interface, broad integration library, and accessibility for non-technical users.

Think of it this way: Make.com is the polished saloon car — comfortable, reliable, and ready to drive off the lot. n8n is the off-road vehicle — more capable, more customisable, but it rewards those who learn to use it properly.

Features: Head to Head

Visual Builder

Make.com has one of the best visual workflow builders available. Your automations display as clear diagrams showing exactly how data flows from one step to the next. It's intuitive, colourful, and easy to follow — even for someone seeing the workflow for the first time.

n8n also uses a visual, node-based builder, but it's more functional than pretty. The canvas-style interface lets you arrange nodes freely, which is better for complex workflows with multiple branches. It's not as visually polished as Make.com, but it's more flexible for advanced use cases.

Winner: Make.com for simplicity. n8n for complex workflows.

Integrations

Make.com offers over 1,500 pre-built integrations. The library covers most popular business tools, and adding new connections is straightforward.

n8n has over 400 built-in integrations — fewer than Make.com. However, n8n has a powerful HTTP Request node that lets you connect to any API, plus community nodes that extend the library significantly. In practice, n8n can connect to anything that has an API.

Winner: Make.com for out-of-the-box breadth. n8n for total flexibility (if you're comfortable with APIs).

Pricing

This is where the platforms diverge significantly.

Make.com charges based on operations (individual steps in your workflows). The free tier gives you 1,000 operations per month. Paid plans start at $9/month for 10,000 operations. A busy automation running 50 times a day with 10 steps each burns through 15,000 operations per month — you'll need a paid plan quickly.

n8n self-hosted is free. You pay only for your server (typically $12-24/month for a VPS). No per-execution limits, no operation caps. Run 1,000 workflows or 100,000 — the cost doesn't change. n8n's cloud option starts at $20/month with generous execution limits.

Winner: n8n, decisively. As you scale, Make.com's costs grow linearly. n8n's don't.

Self-Hosting and Data Privacy

Make.com is cloud-only. Your data passes through Make.com's servers. They have security certifications and data protection policies, but you don't control where your data lives or who has access to the infrastructure.

n8n can be self-hosted on your own infrastructure. Your data never leaves your servers. For businesses dealing with sensitive customer data, financial information, or operating under POPIA (South Africa's data protection law), this is a significant advantage.

Winner: n8n. No contest for data-sensitive businesses.

Ease of Use

Make.com is genuinely easier to learn. The interface guides you through building workflows, the documentation is excellent, and there are templates for common use cases. A non-technical person can build their first automation within an hour.

n8n has a steeper learning curve. The interface is powerful but less hand-holding. Understanding concepts like expressions, data mapping between nodes, and error handling takes more time. That said, once you're comfortable, you can build things in n8n that aren't possible in Make.com.

Winner: Make.com for beginners. n8n rewards the investment in learning.

AI Capabilities

Make.com has added AI features including an AI assistant that helps build workflows and integrations with AI services. It's functional but feels bolted on.

n8n has built AI deeply into the platform. Native nodes for working with large language models, vector databases, AI agents, and document processing. You can build sophisticated AI workflows — RAG systems, intelligent agents, document processors — natively within n8n. The AI integration feels purposeful, not afterthought.

Winner: n8n, by a wide margin. If AI is part of your automation strategy, n8n is the clear choice.

Error Handling and Reliability

Make.com has solid error handling with automatic retries, error routing, and notifications. When something fails, you can see exactly which step broke and why.

n8n offers similar error handling plus more granular control. You can define custom error workflows, implement retry logic with specific delays, and handle partial failures in complex workflows. Self-hosted n8n also means you control the infrastructure reliability.

Winner: Tie for basic needs. n8n for advanced error handling requirements.

Pros and Cons Summary

n8n Pros

  • Self-hosting keeps your data on your servers
  • No per-execution pricing — predictable costs at scale
  • Superior AI capabilities built natively into the platform
  • Full code access when visual builders aren't enough
  • Active open-source community contributing nodes and templates
  • No vendor lock-in — your workflows are portable

n8n Cons

  • Steeper learning curve, especially for non-technical users
  • Fewer pre-built integrations than Make.com or Zapier
  • Self-hosting requires server management knowledge
  • Less polished visual interface

Make.com Pros

  • Best-in-class visual workflow builder
  • Over 1,500 pre-built integrations
  • Easier to learn for non-technical users
  • Excellent documentation and community templates
  • No server management needed

Make.com Cons

  • Cloud-only — no self-hosting option
  • Costs scale linearly with usage (operations-based pricing)
  • Limited AI capabilities compared to n8n
  • Complex workflows can become expensive
  • Less flexibility for custom logic

When to Choose n8n

  • You're working with sensitive data and need to self-host
  • You're building complex, multi-step workflows with custom logic
  • AI automation is part of your strategy
  • You expect high volume and want predictable pricing
  • You're working with an automation partner (like Eight38) who can handle the setup
  • You want to avoid vendor lock-in

When to Choose Make.com

  • You're building your first automations and want the easiest path
  • Your workflows are relatively simple (5-10 steps, few branches)
  • You don't have technical staff and won't be hiring an automation partner
  • Your volume is low and the free tier or basic plan covers your needs
  • You need a specific integration that Make.com has and n8n doesn't

Our Honest Assessment

At Eight38, we've built automations on both platforms. For quick prototypes and simple client workflows, Make.com is excellent. It's fast to build on, easy to demonstrate, and reliable.

But for production automations — the systems that run your business day in, day out — we prefer n8n. The reasons stack up: self-hosting means complete data control. No per-execution charges mean costs stay predictable as clients grow. Native AI capabilities mean we can build intelligent automations that go beyond simple "if-this-then-that" logic. And the code nodes give us an escape hatch when the visual builder isn't enough.

For most of our clients — marketing agencies, SMBs with complex workflows, businesses handling sensitive data — n8n is the better long-term choice. The initial learning curve is steeper, but that's our job to handle. The client gets a more powerful, more cost-effective, more flexible system.

If you're a solo entrepreneur building your first Zap-style automation and you just want something working by lunchtime, start with Make.com. You can always migrate later if you outgrow it.

Still Not Sure?

The best way to decide is to talk through your specific needs. What are you trying to automate? How sensitive is the data? What's your expected volume? These questions determine the right answer for your business — and the answer might not be the same for your competitor.
